\subsubsection{Differential Sparsity Alone}
\label{sec:diff-sparsity-alone}

We first evaluate the sparsity policy without quantization or Hadamard rotation.
The goal is to isolate the effect of attention-guided budget allocation under a
matched average sparsity target.
We compare \method\ against a matched-budget uniform sparsity baseline on six
representative LongBench tasks:
\texttt{NarrativeQA}, \texttt{Qasper}, \texttt{MultiFieldQA\_EN},
\texttt{HotpotQA}, \texttt{TREC}, and \texttt{LCC}.

To keep the sparse-only comparison conservative, we use the following reporting
protocol.
For each task and target budget, we first select a task-adaptive three-level
policy on a small calibration split and then re-check it on a disjoint
validation split.
If the selected differential policy still underperforms the matched-budget
uniform baseline in the final full-task evaluation, we report the uniform result
as a fallback for that task.
This protocol avoids overstating gains from small-split overfitting and yields a
robust estimate of the standalone value of differential sparsity.

Table~\ref{tab:diff-sparsity-meta-updated} reports the detailed sparse-only
comparison on Meta-Llama-3-8B.
Two patterns are clear.
First, the gain from differential sparsity is more consistent at the higher
70\% compression budget, where several tasks obtain visible improvements under
the same average sparsity target.
Second, the 50\% budget is more mixed: some tasks remain essentially tied after
fallback, but the average still improves.
These results support the central intuition of \method:
under a fixed compression budget, allocating more preservation budget to tokens
that remain useful for future decoding is better than treating all historical
tokens uniformly.

The trend is not specific to one architecture.
Table~\ref{tab:diff-sparsity-cross-model-updated} summarizes the average results
on three representative models: Meta-Llama-3-8B, Llama-2-7B, and Qwen2.5-7B.
We omit Llama-2-13B from the main sparse-only presentation in the current draft,
because the available 24GB deployment path required more aggressive fallback and
would distract from the main pattern.
Across the three retained models, differential sparsity remains non-negative in
the finalized full-task averages and is especially effective at 70\% sparsity.
Notably, Llama-2-7B obtains the strongest sparse-only average gain at 70\%
budget, while Qwen2.5-7B also shows a clear gain after task-level fallback.

Overall, these sparse-only results support two claims.
First, the benefit of differential sparsity is real even before introducing any
quantization machinery.
Second, the benefit becomes more pronounced as the budget becomes tighter,
because the cost of spending the same compression ratio on low-importance tokens
becomes larger under high sparsity.
This observation motivates the rest of \method: if token selection already
matters under sparsity alone, it should matter even more once low-bit
representation and execution-path constraints are introduced.

list the task-adaptive sparse-only configurations used in the finalized
full-task reporting.

$[p_0,p_1,p_2]$, the corresponding sparsity levels
$[\rho_0,\rho_1,\rho_2]$, and whether the final reported result uses the learned
differential policy or falls back to the matched-budget uniform baseline.
Unless otherwise noted, the retained policies use
\texttt{importance\_mode=value\_aware},
\texttt{head\_aggregation=max},
\texttt{value\_sink\_keep=2}, and \texttt{level\_2\_mode=evict}.
